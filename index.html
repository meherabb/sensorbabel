Abstract

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) routinely hallucinate physical-world claims — inventing objects, misstating distances, and confabulating velocities — at rates between 20% and 35% on standard benchmarks. Existing fixes either patch the decoding process or require expensive retraining, and both approaches stay entirely inside the model, fighting its biases with more of its own statistics.

We take a different route. Sensor-rich platforms such as autonomous vehicles already carry the instruments that can directly adjudicate the claims MLLMs are most often wrong about. We formalize this idea as sensor authority: the mutual information between a claim's semantic type and a sensor modality's measurement, and prove that physically orthogonal sensors provide a hallucination firewall that is independent of the model's generation process.

Building on this, we introduce SensorBabel, a lightweight four-stage inference-time module — claim extraction, sensor routing, verdict scoring, and contrastive re-weighting — that wraps any frozen MLLM without touching its weights. Evaluated on SensorHalluc, a new benchmark of 4,200 synchronized multi-sensor driving scenes with 25,222 annotated claims, SensorBabel reduces CHAIR S from 72.7 to 27.1 and improves SH-F1 by 34.9 points over an RLHF-finetuned baseline — using only 30,218 trainable parameters and under 200 ms of added latency.

Contributions

What We Introduce

  • 1
    Sensor Authority — Formal Definition. Mutual information between claim semantics and sensor measurements as a routing objective, with three theoretical guarantees: Sensor Independence Firewall (Thm. 3.3), Near-Bayes-Optimal Routing Regret (Thm. 3.4), and Corrected Entropy Bound (Thm. 3.5).
  • 2
    SensorBabel Pipeline. A four-stage inference-time module — claim extraction (Stage 1), sensor routing policy SRP (Stage 2), sensor-specific verdict networks (Stage 3a), and contrastive re-weighting (Stage 3b) — wrapping any frozen MLLM with only 30,218 parameters and zero weight updates.
  • 3
    SensorHalluc Benchmark. 4,200 synchronized multi-sensor driving scenes with 25,222 hallucination-labeled claims across four semantic categories (existence, spatial, motion, material), with a full 20-entry SNR matrix calibrated against physical measurement theory.
  • 4
    Extensive Experiments. Five ablation studies and generalization across four base MLLMs (LLaVA-1.6, InternVL2, Qwen-VL, DriveLM), achieving a consistent ~62.9% average CHAIR S reduction — architecture-agnostic, no model-specific calibration required.
Method

SensorBabel — Architecture Overview

SensorBabel operates entirely at inference time. After an MLLM generates a scene description, the pipeline extracts claims, routes each to its physically authoritative sensor, collects a sensor-specific verdict, and re-weights the decoding distribution. No MLLM weights are ever modified.

Sensor Routing — Physics-Driven Assignment

Each claim type is routed to the sensor whose physics most directly measures its truth condition. These assignments follow measurement physics, not data-driven search — explaining why only 168 labeled scenes are needed to reach near-peak performance.

Key Theoretical Guarantees

Theorem 3.3 — Sensor Independence Firewall

If sensor si is physically orthogonal to the MLLM's generation channel, then as sensor noise ‖εi‖ → 0, the probability of falsely suppressing a true claim → 0 independently of pM. LiDAR (photon time-of-flight) and a language model's token distribution share no statistical dependencies, making their errors provably uncorrelated. Mutually orthogonal sensors further reduce joint error probability → 0.

Theorem 3.4 — Near-Bayes-Optimal Routing Regret

The empirical routing policy π̂ learned from N labeled scenes achieves regret O(√(k log k / N)). With k = 5 sensors, the k log k term is small — explaining why 168 labeled scenes (5% of the benchmark) already reach within 1.1 CHAIR S points of full-data performance. The routing policy saturates quickly because the claim→sensor mapping is grounded in physical structure, not language statistics.

Theorem 3.5 — Corrected Entropy Bound

Under the SensorBabel corrected distribution (Eq. 9), output entropy satisfies H(pSB) ≤ H(pM) · exp(−λ·Δmin). The re-weighting concentrates the decoding distribution around sensor-consistent outputs, with reduction proportional to λ and the minimum expected verdict confidence Δmin.

Ablation V — Data Efficiency

At just 5% of training data (168 labeled scenes), SensorBabel achieves CHAIR S 28.2 and SH-F1 84.4 — within 1.1 pp and 0.8 pp of full-data performance. Annotating 168 scenes (~half a day of driving) is sufficient for near-peak deployment because the claim→sensor routing is grounded in physical law, not discovered from data. The O(1/√N) regret bound (Thm. 3.4) formally guarantees this rapid saturation.

Dataset

SensorHalluc Benchmark

The first benchmark providing synchronized multi-sensor driving data specifically for studying sensor-level hallucination verification in MLLMs. Built on nuScenes conventions with five synchronized modalities and oracle-verified claim labels confirmed by human annotators at 96.4% agreement.

Construction: Each scene is a 20-second segment with LiDAR at 20 Hz, radar at 13 Hz, thermal at 15 Hz, stereo depth at 12 Hz, and IMU at 100 Hz, all aligned to a common 2 Hz annotation keyframe. LLaVA-1.5 generates natural language descriptions; atomic subject–predicate–object claims are extracted by a rule-based parser and verified by modality-specific oracle classifiers: PointNet++ for existence (3×3 m bounding box), calibrated depth estimator for spatial, Doppler radar for motion velocity, thermal segmentation for material emissivity. Human annotators confirmed 96.4% agreement with oracle labels on 500 randomly sampled claims. Train/test split: 80/20 (3,360 / 840 scenes), stratified by hallucination rate ±1% across splits.
